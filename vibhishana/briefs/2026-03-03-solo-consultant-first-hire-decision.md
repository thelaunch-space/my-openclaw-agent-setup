## ICP Problem Translation

**What they're actually asking:**

"I'm a solo consultant making good money ($250K-$500K) but turning down work or burning out. Everyone says 'hire when you have consistent overflow,' but I don't know if I can afford it, what systems I need first, or whether to hire full-time vs. use contractors. I'm scared of:
- Destroying my 70-80% margins
- Managing someone poorly and wasting 6 months
- Hiring too early before I have processes documented
- Losing client trust when they see 'junior' people on their project"

**Why this matters now:**

1. **Post-2020 solo consulting boom hitting capacity walls** - Many consultants who went solo during pandemic are now 3-4 years in, hitting $300K+, facing the "do I stay solo or build a team?" decision
2. **AI is NOT solving the capacity problem** - ChatGPT helps with deliverables, but client calls, relationship management, and custom strategy still require human hours
3. **Margin compression reality check** - Generic advice says "hire at $300K" but doesn't explain the 30-50% margin hit that happens, causing panic when it arrives
4. **Contractor economy maturity** - Upwork/Fiverr have normalized high-quality subcontracting, but consultants don't know how to structure these deals properly

**Bookmark potential:** This would be the reference guide for the $200K-$500K solo consultant making the hire/don't hire decision. Includes actual math, decision frameworks, and contractor playbook.

## Suggested Structure

**H1: When to Hire Your First Employee as a Solo Consultant (And Why Most Do It Too Early)**

**H2: Why the "$300K Revenue" Rule Exists (The Real Math)**
- The solo capacity ceiling: billable hours × rate = revenue max
- Example calculation: $200/hr × 1,500 hours = $300K (your actual limit)
- Below $300K? Raise your rates first, don't hire

**H2: The Margin Compression Nobody Warns You About**
- Solo margins: 70-80% (just your time + minimal overhead)
- First hire margins: 30-50% (their cost + management time + overhead)
- The psychological shock: "I'm making less money with more revenue"
- Breakeven analysis: how much revenue must grow to maintain take-home

**H2: Contractor-First: The $2K Test Before the $70K Commitment**
- Why your first "hire" should be a project-based contractor
- Where to find them: Upwork, specialized networks, referrals
- How to vet: 5-point screening framework
- Deal structures: percentage split vs. hourly, IP clauses, NDAs
- Red flags: ghosting, quality issues, client detection
- When to convert contractor to employee: 3 signals of readiness

**H2: The 3 Systems You MUST Document Before Hiring Anyone**
- System 1: Client intake (from lead to signed contract)
- System 2: Delivery workflow (project kickoff to final deliverable)
- System 3: Invoicing and payment (billing cycles, collections)
- How much documentation is enough? The 2-week onboarding test
- Templates > manuals: give them fillable templates, not 50-page PDFs

**H2: The Decision Framework: Should You Actually Hire?**
- Capacity test: current billable hours, max sustainable, overflow per month
- Affordability test: 6 months low utilization while they ramp (not just 9 months salary)
- Bottleneck diagnosis: are you turning down delivery work, or drowning in admin?
- Role prioritization matrix: hire for client delivery vs. admin vs. sales
- The burnout trap: don't hire to fix burnout, hire to capture overflow revenue

**H2: What Changes When You Bring on Your First Team Member**
- Client communication: "You're still my main contact, right?" (How to introduce without losing trust)
- Pricing adjustments: raise rates? Team-based pricing? Project minimums?
- Quality control: ensure standards without micromanaging (QA checklists, spot reviews)
- Time allocation shift: 10-20 hours/week on management (account for this in capacity math)

**H2: Failure Scenarios and How to Avoid Them**
- Hired too early: paying idle capacity, cash burn
- Hired wrong person: 3-6 month recovery, client damage control
- Hired before systems: spend all time training, margins collapse
- How to course-correct: probation periods, clear performance metrics, exit plans

**H2: The Thresholds Checklist: Are You Actually Ready?**
- [ ] Revenue: consistently $300K+ annualized for 6+ months
- [ ] Overflow: turning down $10K+ in work monthly
- [ ] Systems: 3 core processes documented (intake, delivery, invoicing)
- [ ] Cash reserves: 6 months operating expenses + 6 months new hire salary
- [ ] Role clarity: know exactly what bottleneck you're hiring for
- [ ] Test completed: ran $2K+ contractor project successfully
- [ ] Client readiness: can introduce team without trust loss

**H2: When to Stay Solo Instead**
- You're under $200K revenue: raise rates, improve positioning first
- Margins matter more than growth: solo 80% margins > team 35% margins if lifestyle is goal
- You love the work: hiring means becoming a manager, losing hands-on time
- Project-based income: inconsistent revenue makes payroll risky
