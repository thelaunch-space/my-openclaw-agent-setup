### Angle 2: "The Adoption Pyramid"

**insightText:** The Adoption Pyramid inverts how service businesses evaluate CRMs. Layer 1 (will your team open it daily?) is the foundation. Layer 2 (does it capture the 3 essentials: lead source, last contact, next action?) comes next. Layer 3 (automation, reporting, integrations) only matters if Layers 1 and 2 are solid. Most CRM selection starts at Layer 3. That is why 50-70% of implementations fail.

**rationale:** A 48-year-old legal or healthcare services founder is shopping for a CRM based on feature comparison charts: which has better automation? more integrations? advanced reporting? This flips the evaluation - start with adoption, not features. A Notion database their team updates religiously beats a $100/month enterprise CRM nobody touches. For time-poor service business owners billing $200-500/hour, this prevents the 3-month cycle of setup → abandonment → back to spreadsheets. Bookmark-worthy because it changes HOW they shop, not just WHAT they buy.

**hookOptions:**
1. "A Notion database your team uses religiously beats a $100/month CRM nobody touches."
2. "You are evaluating CRMs by features. You should be evaluating by daily adoption first."
3. "Most service founders pick CRMs by comparing automation and reporting features. Then their team abandons it in 3 months."
4. "The CRM selection process is backwards. Layer 1 is adoption. Features come last."

### Angle 3: "The 5-Person Inflection Point"

**insightText:** Service businesses hit the CRM inflection point at exactly 4-5 people. At 1-3 people, spreadsheets and email work fine - everything is in the founder's head. At 4-5 people, chaos emerges: multiple people talk to prospects, follow-ups get missed, nobody has complete visibility, leads slip through cracks. The data confirms it: 91% of companies with 10+ employees use CRM vs only 50% of teams under 10. The gap is real.

**rationale:** A 45-year-old consultancy or clinic owner is at exactly 4-5 people right now and feeling this pain: who last contacted that warm lead? did anyone follow up after the discovery call? This names the exact moment they are living through and validates it as a universal pattern, not their personal failure. For a founder wondering "should I get a CRM or just work harder on tracking," this gives them permission to solve it with systems instead of willpower. Pattern recognition makes them stop scrolling and think "that is exactly where I am."
